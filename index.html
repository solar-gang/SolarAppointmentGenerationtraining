Bill Knowledge / Problem

Most important

Your goal: explain where charges come from, how TOU drives pain, how tiers punish heavy users, and why solar billing (NEM) can look confusing. If a rep can teach this clearly, the consult books itself.

Tap to learn
1) Usage page: the “truth” of the home
Find kWh
  • Always find the page that shows monthly usage (kWh) and trends.
  • Use the daily average to frame lifestyle: EVs, pool pumps, AC, electrification.
  • Non-negotiable: screenshot or collect usage every time. No usage = no responsible design.
Quick line (1 sentence): “Before we talk solar, we need your usage—that’s the only way to design something that saves you day one.”
2) Tiers + baseline: why heavy users pay more per kWh
Tier 1 vs Tier 2
  • Tier 1 = within baseline allowance (cheaper).
  • Tier 2 = over baseline (more expensive).
  • Simple teaching: “The more you use, the higher the price per kWh.”
Rep move: connect tiers to growth EV + pool + kids + AC → usage grows → you live in Tier 2.
Close the gap: why consult matters “Let’s design around your real usage so you stop paying premium rates.”
3) Time-of-Use (TOU): peak window is where the bill gets expensive
4–9pm story
  • Show that price varies by time (peak vs off-peak).
  • Explain demand drivers: EV charging, electrification, high grid load.
  • Battery explanation: store energy (or solar) and use it during peak instead of buying peak power.
Quick line: “TOU means it’s not just how much you use—it’s when you use it, and 4–9pm is the expensive window.”
4) Delivery vs Generation: where charges are coming from
Two buckets
  • Delivery = poles/wires, transmission, distribution, public programs, etc.
  • Generation = cost to produce/purchase electricity.
  • Teaching point: even if one bucket changes, the other can rise—why bills still climb.
Make it simple: “Delivery is the highway. Generation is the gas. You get charged for both.”
Appointment angle: “We’ll review your bill line by line and show the cleanest path to day-one savings.”
5) Solar billing / NEM: why bills can look confusing
True-up
  • Many solar customers have an annual settlement (often called a true-up).
  • The bill can show small monthly amounts while credits/debits track across the year.
  • Key message: “We design to keep you protected during peak and reduce your net cost.”
Quick line: “Solar billing can look weird month to month—what matters is the full picture across the year and the true-up.”
6) Rep checklist: what to collect every time
Non-negotiable
  • Usage (kWh) for the most recent month + trend if available
  • Rate plan (TOU plan name)
  • Peak window (when it’s expensive)
  • Any tiers/baseline info
  • Delivery vs Generation summary
  • Solar/NEM status if they already have solar
Field habit: Ask: “Do you pay online?” If yes, they can pull the bill instantly and you can screenshot the key pages.
Appointment close (30 seconds): “If you’re open to it, we can do a quick 30–45 minute consult where we review your bill, confirm your usage, and show whether solar + batteries gives you day-one savings. If it doesn’t save you immediately, we’ll tell you and stop there.”

Approach

Trust + control

The goal at the door is not to explain everything. The goal is to earn the right to sit down. Your delivery (tone, pace, eye contact) is more important than your words.

Door approach
Door Approach Structure (Enzy style)
Intro → Problem → Solution
  • Intro: who you are + why you’re here (short)
  • Problem: TOU/peak + rising rates + lack of control
  • Solution: solar + battery = store energy + avoid peak
  • Takeaways: day-one savings (if qualified) + outage protection + control
  • Transition: “Let’s schedule the consult so we can verify savings.”
  • Assume sale: offer two time options
Two-option close: “Would today around 6:30 work, or is tomorrow better—like 4:00?”
Tone + pace (slow down to sound expensive)
Control
  • Start calm and slightly slower than normal.
  • Use pauses after key points (peak window, bill pain, consult ask).
  • Ups/downs matter when you ask questions; steady confidence for statements.
Appearance + body language
Trust signals
  • Clean, simple, professional. Don’t look “salesy.”
  • Hands visible, relaxed. Don’t point aggressively.
  • Eye contact: hold during questions, soften during listening.
  • Hand motions: slow and intentional, not fast and nervous.
Prospecting homes + ideal schedule
Consistency
  • Target: EVs, pools, older AC, larger homes, high usage neighborhoods.
  • Knock consistent time blocks (same days, same hours) to build rhythm.
  • Track doors → track contacts → track set appointments (simple KPI flow).
Reminder: As soon as someone shows interest, transition to collecting usage/bill and booking the consult.
Approach rule: “You’re not there to convince. You’re there to educate enough to earn the sit-down.”
